The strategic read — beyond the price promise
toing isn't just cheaper. It's Swiggy reclaiming the habit it lost.
The deeper story here: as Swiggy optimised for higher AOV users, it quietly abandoned the ₹100–₹150 order — the everyday, habitual, impulse transaction that makes a platform indispensable. Platforms that win the ₹100 order don't just win a transaction. They win the morning chai routine, the midday lunch default, the late-night snack impulse. They win habit. toing is Swiggy's attempt to recapture that frequency cohort before Zomato or Rapido locks it in permanently. The central question isn't whether toing is cheaper — pricing is a variable. It's whether higher margins per order and higher frequency per user can coexist in the same platform ecosystem. That's the real experiment. And the ops and product infrastructure needed to run it at scale is exactly where I'd want to work.
What toing is really doing
Winning the frequency cohort before Zomato or Rapido does
4M+ downloads in 7 months, 23 cities, 4.5 rating. toing's lower pricing is engineered through deliberate structural constraints — tighter delivery radius, curated catalogue, standardised experience — that reduce cost-to-serve enough to offer restaurant menu prices sustainably. This isn't a discount strategy. It's a structural redesign of the food delivery model for the high-frequency, price-sensitive user.
The ops problem I'd work on
Price integrity at scale — keeping the promise as cities multiply
App reviews flag ₹30-40 charges appearing at checkout that weren't visible upfront. As toing hits 23+ cities, maintaining accurate, fee-free pricing across thousands of restaurant menus is a data and ops infrastructure problem. Price integrity can't be a manual check — it has to be a system. Building exactly this kind of automated quality system is what I've been doing at Bridgetown.
